Korean who fought for Three armys

Yang Kyoungjong was a Korean soldier during the Second World War who found himself fighting under the Japanese, the Soviet Union, and Nazi Germany, by the time the conflict was over Yang enlisted into the Japanese army as young as eighteen years of age, though not by choice. He fought for the Japanese for all of a year before the Soviet Union captured and imprisoned him. When the Soviet Union found themselves struggling in 1942, they required the Korean soldier to fight for them. He and a vast array of other prisoners found themselves fighting for the other side, but this again would not last long. He had another year-long stint in the military before once again being captured, this time by the Germans. By 1944, Yang was fighting for the German side. Again, those he fought alongside were predomi nantly other prisoners. He managed to survive until his eventual imprisonment by Allied forces. By that time, the war was close enough to over that the Korean soldier was not forced to fight any further. Still, he fought for three armies in a war that did not spare many men who only f ought for one. Even worse, he was never fighting for his own beliefs, but always for those thrust upon him.

The P.1000 Ratte

The P.1000 Ratte was a proposed super heavy tank by Nazi scientist Krupp. the Ratte was supposed to be armed with 280mm naval cannons and was to weight 1000 tons, making it the heaviest tank ever created. the design for the tank came from studying Soviet heavy tanks, which also gave rise to the Panzer VIII Maus, the current heaviest tank ever built, at 188 tons. The large size and weight would have rendered the tank unable to cross bridges at the risk of collapsing them, and travelling on roads would soon destroy them. Though its top intended speed was 40 kilometres per hour, its huge size and high visibility would have made it extremely vulnerable to aerial bombardment and artillery fire.
